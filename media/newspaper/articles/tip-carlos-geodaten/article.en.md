# Traces in a photo – how to read images properly

Hi, I'm Carlos from the tech section of our school newspaper.

Recently a friend suddenly couldn't be reached.  
All we had was a photo.

So we looked at the image very carefully.  
And it became clear: a photo often shows more than you'd think at first glance.

👉 Sometimes an image reveals not just **what** is visible.  
It also reveals **where**, **when** and **how** it was taken.

[[callout kind="info"]]
When you take a photo with your phone, the device often saves extra information alongside it.

This information is called **metadata**.

For example:
- 📍 Location
- 🕒 Time
- 📅 Date
- 📱 Device type
- 🧭 Camera orientation

You can also spot clues directly in the image itself.  
These visible traces are often called **geodata** or **context clues**.
[[/callout]]

## What you can find in images

Some clues are visible in the image itself.

For example:
- 🏔 Landscape
- 🪧 Signs
- 🏫 Logos
- 🌤 Shadows
- 🪟 Reflections


You can also upload images into a search engine to find them.  
Sometimes you'll find similar images on the web.

This is called a **Reverse Image Search**.

[[img src="media/newspaper/articles/tip-carlos-geodaten/geodaten1" alt="Example image with visible clues"]]

[[details title="🔍 What clues are hidden in the image?"]]
- 📍 Location saved
- 🪧 Street sign visible
- 🏫 School logo on clothing
- 🕒 Shadow roughly shows the time of day
- 🚲 distinctive sticker
- 😊 Face recognisable
[[/details]]

## When this can be useful

Metadata isn't automatically bad.

It can also be helpful.

For example:
- Finding photos more easily
- Finding places again
- Sorting images better
- Placing memories more quickly

👉 Metadata is a bit like small sticky notes in the background.


[[img src="media/newspaper/articles/tip-carlos-geodaten/geodaten2" alt="Example of location display"]]

## When it becomes risky

It becomes problematic when too much information comes together.

Then it's sometimes possible to find out more from a photo than you'd like.

[[details title="⚠️ What can become visible?"]]
- your face
- your location
- the time
- your habits

It's often the combination that makes it tricky.
[[/details]]

[[callout kind="warn"]]
No single detail is the issue.

It often becomes risky when **face + location + time + habit** all match up.
[[/callout]]

## Facial recognition – why this matters

Faces are particularly sensitive information.

Because they can be connected to other data.

[[details title="🔎 Case 1: Linked to your name"]]
Systems can connect faces with names or profiles.

A single photo is often not yet the problem.

But many images together can build a much more detailed profile.
[[/details]]

[[img src="media/newspaper/articles/tip-carlos-geodaten/geodaten3" alt="Example facial recognition"]]


[[details title="🎭 Case 2: Deepfakes"]]
Faces can today be inserted into other images or videos.

This can look very real, even though it's completely made up.

That's why it's important to be careful with images.
[[/details]]

[[img src="media/newspaper/articles/tip-carlos-geodaten/geodaten4" alt="Example deepfake"]]

[[callout kind="info"]]
Just because something is technically possible doesn't mean it's okay or allowed.
[[/callout]]

[[callout kind="tip"]]
A good start is:
- Share photos only with people you trust
- Consciously turn location on or off
- Before posting, briefly think about what the image reveals
[[/callout]]

## How to set your location

[[details title="📱 iPhone"]]
Settings → Privacy & Security → Location Services → Camera
[[/details]]

[[details title="🤖 Android"]]
Settings → Location → Camera
[[/details]]

## How to view metadata

[[details title="⚙️ How to find it"]]
Open photo → tap "i" or swipe up

There you'll often see:
- Location
- Date
- Device
[[/details]]

## Mini-check before sharing

[[callout kind="tip"]]
Ask yourself before posting:

- Would I show this publicly?
- Is the location visible?
- Is my face clearly recognisable?
- Can habits be derived from this?
- Does the location really need to be included?
[[/callout]]

In the end, it's not about restrictions.

It's about consciously deciding  
which traces you want to leave with a photo.
